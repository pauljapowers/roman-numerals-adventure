Roman Republic – 500 Years

Cato introduces the Republic: “Now Rome decides that no single king should rule. Citizens vote, senators debate, and heroes write letters home from far‑away wars. Welcome to five hundred years of the Roman Republic!”

The Roman Republic lasted for about 500 years. During this time, Romans experimented with new ways of sharing power. Ordinary citizens had more of a voice, but life was still very different for rich and poor people. Many famous stories, wars, and leaders come from this period.

1. Republic Government

In the Roman Republic, leaders were chosen by citizens instead of crowned as kings. There were two main officials called consuls who shared power and could stop each other’s decisions. A group called the Senate advised them, and different assemblies allowed citizens to vote on some laws.

2. Comparing the US and Roman Republic Governments

Like the Roman Republic, the United States has elected leaders, a senate, and written laws. Both systems try to balance power so no single person controls everything. However, many people in Rome could not vote, including women and enslaved people, while modern democracies try to give more citizens a voice.

3. SPQR

The letters SPQR stand for Senatus Populusque Romanus, which means “The Senate and People of Rome”. Romans carved SPQR on buildings, flags, and shields to show that government came from both the leaders and the citizens.

4. The Twelve Tables

Early in the Republic, Romans wrote down their laws on bronze tablets called the Twelve Tables. These tablets were displayed in the Forum so that everyone could see the rules. This helped stop powerful people from secretly changing laws to suit themselves.

5. Crime and Justice in Ancient Rome

Romans took law and order seriously. People accused of crimes could sometimes speak in their own defence or have a lawyer. Punishments included fines, exile from the city, or, for serious crimes, death. Rich people often had better chances in court than poor people.

6. Daily Life for Patricians

Patricians were wealthy families with large houses and many slaves. They enjoyed fancy dinners, political meetings, and trips to the baths. Their children sometimes had private tutors who taught them reading, writing, Greek, and public speaking.

7. Daily Life for Plebeians

Plebeians were ordinary citizens such as farmers, shopkeepers, and craftsmen. Many lived in crowded apartment buildings. They worked hard during the day and relaxed by visiting the market, the baths, or the games in the arena when they could.

8. Daily Life in the Countryside

Outside the city, many Romans lived on farms. Families grew grain, olives, grapes, and raised animals like sheep and goats. Life followed the seasons: planting, harvesting, and caring for crops. News from Rome sometimes arrived only after many days.

9. Roman Houses and the Atrium

Wealthy Romans lived in houses with an open central hall called an atrium. Rainwater fell through a hole in the roof into a pool on the floor. Rooms opened around the atrium, and guests could admire family shrines, statues, and colourful wall paintings.

10. Clothing & Hair Styles

Roman citizens often wore a simple tunic, and on special days men might add a toga, a large draped cloth. Women wore long dresses called stolae and styled their hair in braids or curls. Wealthy people might use pins and jewellery to show their status.

11. Bulla

Roman children were given a small locket called a bulla to wear around their necks. It contained lucky charms to protect them from harm. Boys usually wore their bulla until they became adults and put on a man’s toga.

12. The Forum

During the Republic, the Forum was busier than ever. Citizens met there to trade, listen to court cases, and hear speeches from politicians. If you stood in the Forum, you might hear arguments about new laws or news from distant provinces.

13. The Baths

Public bath houses were like a mix of a swimming pool, gym, and social club. Romans could exercise, wash in hot and cold pools, and relax with friends. Many baths also had libraries and snack stalls, making them popular meeting places.

14. Occupations and Jobs

Romans worked in many different jobs: farmers, soldiers, builders, merchants, scribes, and more. Some people ran small shops, while others worked on large estates or in government offices. Enslaved people did much of the hardest labour.

15. Kids & School

Many Roman children learned at home or in small schools run by teachers. They practised writing on wax tablets, memorised poems, and learned multiplication with counting boards. Good handwriting and clear speaking were very important skills.

16. Wedding Customs

Roman weddings included special clothes, music, and feasts. Brides wore a bright orange veil and carried a small torch. After the ceremony, guests shouted good wishes as the couple walked to their new home.

17. Roman Mosaics

Romans decorated floors and walls with mosaics, pictures made from tiny pieces of coloured stone or glass. Mosaics showed animals, gods, and scenes from everyday life. They were beautiful and also easier to clean than painted floors.

18. Roman Legion

During the Republic, the legion became Rome’s main fighting force. Citizens served as soldiers, training to march in formation and use shields and swords. Successful generals gained great fame and sometimes used their armies to win political power.

19. Roman Letters Home & Inscriptions

Soldiers and travellers sometimes wrote letters home on thin wooden tablets or papyrus. Important messages and honours were carved into stone inscriptions. Archaeologists still discover these writings today, giving us a peek into real Roman voices.

20. Roman Roads

As Rome conquered more land, it built sturdy roads to move soldiers and goods. These roads connected new provinces to the city of Rome. Mile‑stones along the way told travellers how far they were from the capital.

21. Provinces & Expansion

Lands outside Italy that Rome controlled were called provinces. Governors were sent to collect taxes and keep order. Some provinces became rich from trade in grain, metals, or wine, but others suffered from heavy taxation and unfair treatment.

22. The Romans in Britain

During the late Republic and early Empire, Roman armies reached the island of Britain. They built forts, roads, and walls, including the famous Hadrian’s Wall. Roman rule brought new foods, coins, and baths, but also battles with local tribes.

23. The Aeneid by Virgil

The poet Virgil wrote an epic poem called the Aeneid. It tells the story of Aeneas, a brave Trojan who travels to Italy and becomes an ancestor of the Romans. The poem linked Rome’s past to the heroic tales of the Trojan War and gave the Republic a grand, mythical origin.

24. Hannibal & the Punic Wars

Rome fought three long wars against the city of Carthage, called the Punic Wars. The Carthaginian general Hannibal became famous for leading war elephants across the Alps to surprise the Romans. After many battles, Rome finally won and became the strongest power in the western Mediterranean.

25. Julius Caesar

Julius Caesar was a brilliant general who conquered Gaul and became extremely popular. He used his army to gain control of Rome and was named “dictator for life”. Some senators feared he would make himself king, so they plotted against him and assassinated him on the Ides of March.

26. Cicero

Cicero was a famous Roman lawyer and speaker who loved the ideals of the Republic. He wrote many letters and speeches about justice, friendship, and good government. His words are still studied today by students learning about rhetoric and philosophy.

27. The Republic Fails

By the end of the Republic, Rome was full of problems: rich and poor citizens argued, generals used their armies to win power, and civil wars broke out. After Caesar’s death, his adopted heir Octavian defeated his rivals and became the first emperor, Augustus. The Republic was over, and the Empire had begun.
