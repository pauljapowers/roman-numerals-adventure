Roman Kingdom – 200 Years

Cato welcomes you: “This chapter tells the earliest stories of Rome. From wolf‑raised twins to brave heroes on bridges, the Roman Kingdom is where it all began!”

Historians call the first part of Rome’s story the Roman Kingdom. It lasted for about 200 years and was ruled by a line of kings. Some of these kings may be legendary, but their stories help us understand what early Romans admired: bravery, cleverness, and respect for the gods.

1. How Ancient Romans Differed from Ancient Greeks

The Romans admired the Greeks, but they were not the same. Greek cities loved art, ideas, and debates. Romans liked these things too, but they also focused on order, laws, and building a strong army. Greeks told stories about heroes who searched for glory, while Romans praised leaders who served their city and kept their promises.

2. Geography, Natural Resources, Maps

Rome grew beside the River Tiber in central Italy. The city was surrounded by seven hills, which made it easier to defend. Nearby fields provided grain, olives, and grapes for food and trade. Because Italy sticks out into the Mediterranean Sea like a boot, Roman ships could easily reach many other lands.

3. The Etruscans

Before Rome became powerful, a people called the Etruscans lived in northern and central Italy. They were skilled metalworkers and builders. Romans learned many things from them, including how to build arches and how to read certain signs from the gods. Some of Rome’s early kings were probably Etruscan.

4. Romulus & Remus

According to legend, twin brothers Romulus and Remus were abandoned as babies and rescued by a she‑wolf. When they grew up, they decided to build a city. A fierce argument followed, and Romulus killed Remus. Romulus named the new city Rome after himself and became its first king.

5. Gods & Goddesses

Early Romans believed that spirits and gods watched over every part of life: the home, the fields, and the sky. Later, they matched many of their gods with Greek ones. For example, the Roman god Jupiter was similar to the Greek god Zeus, and Venus was similar to Aphrodite.

6. Roman Council of 12 Gods

The most important Roman gods were sometimes called the “Council of Twelve”. These included Jupiter, Juno, Minerva, Mars, Venus, Mercury, Neptune, Diana, Apollo, Vesta, Ceres, and Vulcan. Temples and festivals were held in their honour, and people asked them for protection and good fortune.

7. Roman Myths

Roman myths told exciting stories about brave heroes, magical creatures, and powerful gods. These tales explained why storms happened, how cities were founded, and what it meant to be loyal and brave. Children in Rome grew up hearing these stories just as you might hear fairy tales today.

8. How the Planets Got Their Names

Many of the planets in our solar system are named after Roman gods. Mars, the red planet, is named after the god of war. Venus, the bright evening star, is named after the goddess of love and beauty. Even the planet Neptune, deep blue in colour, is named after the god of the sea.

9. Patricians & Plebeians

Early Roman society was divided into two main groups: patricians and plebeians. Patricians were wealthy families who owned land and often became leaders. Plebeians were ordinary citizens such as farmers, craft workers, and traders. Both groups were important for building Rome.

10. Pater Familias & Family

In each Roman household, the pater familias was the head of the family, usually the oldest man. He was expected to care for everyone, make decisions, and honour the family’s ancestors. Children were taught to obey their parents and to show respect to their elders.

11. Horatius at the Bridge

One famous story from the time of the Roman Kingdom is about Horatius Cocles. When an enemy army tried to cross a bridge into Rome, Horatius stood at the far end and fought them off while the bridge was destroyed behind him. He risked his life to protect his city, and Romans remembered him as a model of courage and loyalty.
